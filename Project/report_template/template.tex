\section*{Reverse-Hoops-Playing Robot Arm}

\subsection*{Setup and Experiment Design}

The project asks the robot to move a hoop so that a flying ball passes through it. I treat the problem as one estimation and constrained planning pipeline. The system receives noisy Cartesian ball measurements, predicts the future ball path, selects candidate catch points, solves a UR10 trajectory optimization problem, and checks whether the ball center crosses the hoop plane inside the physical opening.

I use the four project tasks as separate experiments. Task 1 tests prediction error. Task 2 creates a simple geometric baseline. Task 3 tests whether the UR10 can execute a constrained hoop motion. Task 4 compares candidate-selection policies on the same predicted trajectories and the same success definition.

\subsection*{Task 1: Predicting the Ball}

The ball state uses position and velocity,
\[
x=\begin{bmatrix}p_x&p_y&p_z&v_x&v_y&v_z\end{bmatrix}^\top .
\]
The process model integrates constant horizontal velocity and known gravity in \(z\). The measurement model observes position only. Under these assumptions, a linear Kalman filter gives the exact Gaussian Bayesian update for a linear system with Gaussian process and measurement noise. This is the main reason for using it. An EKF would reduce to the same linear update, and a particle filter would add sampling noise to a six-dimensional problem that already has an analytic covariance update.

The validation reports measurement error, filtered error, future-prediction error, and covariance validity. Over 50 seeds at the project noise level, the raw measurement RMSE was \(1.74\,\mathrm{mm}\), the filtered RMSE was \(0.90\,\mathrm{mm}\), and the 0.5 s future-prediction RMSE was \(3.51\,\mathrm{mm}\). The 95th percentile future-prediction RMSE was \(6.35\,\mathrm{mm}\), far below the \(30\,\mathrm{mm}\) hoop clearance.

    \caption{Kalman-filter prediction for one seed. The future prediction continues the gravity-driven parabola from the filtered state.}

I also tested whether this conclusion depends on the exact 1 mm noise value. Figure~\ref{fig:noise} sweeps measurement noise from 1 to 10 mm. The mean 0.5 s prediction RMSE rises from \(3.67\) to \(10.34\,\mathrm{mm}\), and the maximum observed RMSE remains \(20.01\,\mathrm{mm}\). The predictor still stays below the hoop clearance in this sweep. This does not prove arbitrary robustness, but it shows that the filter has useful margin around the stated simulator noise.

\subsection*{Task 2: Simple Interception Point}

Task 2 asks for a simple interception strategy, so I deliberately use a geometric rule instead of robot optimization. The selector samples the predicted trajectory and rejects points that occur too soon, sit below \(z=0.35\,\mathrm{m}\), or lie too far from the current hoop center. It then chooses the earliest remaining point. For the validation seed, this rule selected \(t=1.36\,\mathrm{s}\) and position \([1.224,-0.119,1.074]\,\mathrm{m}\).

This baseline has a clear purpose: it answers where the ball could be caught in Cartesian space. It does not answer whether the UR10 can move the hoop there quickly enough. The later benchmark keeps this baseline honest. Across 50 seeds, the simple selector attempts an early catch at \(1.295\,\mathrm{s}\) on average, but none of those attempts pass the full NLP and hoop-crossing check. The attempted time is useful as a lower bound, not as a successful interception time.

\subsection*{Task 3: Optimization-Based UR10 Control}

The controller solves a finite-horizon nonlinear program over joint position, velocity, and acceleration,
\[
\{q_k,\dot q_k\}_{k=0}^{N},\quad \{\ddot q_k\}_{k=0}^{N-1}.
\]
It uses the discrete second-order dynamics
\[
q_{k+1}=q_k+\dot q_k\Delta t+\tfrac{1}{2}\ddot q_k\Delta t^2,\qquad
\dot q_{k+1}=\dot q_k+\ddot q_k\Delta t .
\]
The optimization problem is
\[
\begin{aligned}
\min_{q,\dot q,\ddot q}\;&
w_p\|p_{\mathrm{tcp}}(q_N)-p^\star\|_2^2
+\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} w_u\|\ddot q_k\|_2^2
+\sum_{k=1}^{N} w_v\|\dot q_k\|_2^2
+w_o(1-R_{zz}(q_N))^2\\
\mathrm{s.t.}\;& q_0=q_{\mathrm{init}},\quad \dot q_0=\dot q_{\mathrm{init}},\\
& q_{\min}\le q_k\le q_{\max},\quad
-\dot q_{\max}\le \dot q_k\le \dot q_{\max},\quad
-\ddot q_{\max}\le \ddot q_k\le \ddot q_{\max},\\
& z_{\mathrm{tcp}}(q_k)\ge h_{\mathrm{table}}+m_{\mathrm{tcp}} .
\end{aligned}
\]
The validation uses \(N=28\), \(\Delta t=0.05\,\mathrm{s}\), \(w_p=1500\), \(w_u=2\times 10^{-2}\), \(w_v=10^{-3}\), and \(w_o=10\). Pinocchio supplies the URDF-based forward kinematics, including the TCP position and rotation. CasADi builds the symbolic nonlinear program, and IPOPT solves it.

For the Task 2 target, the NLP reduced the hoop-target distance from \(0.731\,\mathrm{m}\) to \(0.020\,\mathrm{m}\). It solved in \(0.075\,\mathrm{s}\) and 14 IPOPT iterations on the test machine. The maximum joint acceleration reached \(1.9996\,\mathrm{rad/s^2}\), and the maximum velocity ratio was \(0.640\). The planned trajectory respected all position, velocity, and acceleration bounds.

The safety checks support the same conclusion. The minimum TCP-table clearance was \(0.622\,\mathrm{m}\), the minimum frame-table clearance was \(0.115\,\mathrm{m}\), and the minimum self-sphere clearance was \(0.163\,\mathrm{m}\). These checks do not replace exact mesh collision detection. They give conservative evidence that the submitted motion does not use a visibly unsafe configuration.

\subsection*{Task 4: Smarter Interception}

Task 4 asks for a smarter strategy that improves success rate and interception time. I do not compare the final strategy against a successful Task 2 time, because the simple strategy has no successful catches under the full criterion. Instead, I report the Task 2 attempted time and then evaluate feasible strategies that can actually catch the ball.

The smarter strategy changes the decision rule, not the simulator. It forms a shared candidate pool from the predicted trajectory, runs the Task 3 NLP for each shortlisted candidate, and accepts only candidates that satisfy three conditions: the NLP succeeds, the ball crosses the hoop plane, and the radial crossing error stays within \(0.03\,\mathrm{m}\). The time-first feasible policy then selects the earliest candidate that passes these tests. I also report a balanced-cost policy as an ablation. It uses the same candidate evaluations but trades extra catch time for lower radial error and lower control usage.

I added a small pose-aware term for Task 4 candidate evaluation. The term encourages the hoop normal to align with the local ball-flight direction through \(w_n(1-(n_{\mathrm{hoop}}^\top \hat v_{\mathrm{ball}})^2)\), with \(w_n=5\). This term follows the physical success metric: a ball passes through a hoop opening, not just through a point in space. A larger weight reduced success in tuning, so I kept it as a weak geometric preference rather than a dominant objective.

The main Task 4 result is the time-first feasible policy. It raises the full-system success rate from 0\% for the simple baseline to 96\%, while preserving the earliest successful catch among evaluated candidates. The balanced-cost policy keeps the same success rate and improves radial error from \(9.21\) to \(3.62\,\mathrm{mm}\), but it catches later. This experiment directly addresses the earlier ambiguity: the submitted ``smarter'' answer for time is the feasibility-aware time-first policy, and the balanced policy demonstrates the tradeoff rather than replacing the time objective.

\subsection*{Failure Analysis and Limits}

Two of the 50 seeds fail for the time-first feasible policy. Figure~\ref{fig:failure} shows why. Their candidate errors remain outside the \(30\,\mathrm{mm}\) crossing tolerance until the latest tested candidates, and the candidate pool ends before a robust feasible crossing appears. This is a planning-window and candidate-pool limitation, not a Kalman-filter failure.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.58\linewidth]{../outputs/high_score/figure_failure_diagnostics.png}
    \caption{Failure-case diagnosis for seeds 11 and 41. The radial crossing error remains above tolerance for the evaluated feasible window.}
    \label{fig:failure}
\end{figure}

The simulator includes a ground bounce model. The Kalman model used here assumes pre-bounce ballistic flight. If a selected trajectory crosses a bounce, the estimator would need a switching model or an interacting multiple-model filter. I keep the submitted experiments in the pre-bounce catching regime. The collision evidence also remains approximate, because the implementation checks table clearance and sphere-based self-clearance rather than exact mesh collision.

\subsection*{Conclusion}

The final system gives a complete offline solution for the reverse-hoops task. The Kalman filter predicts the ball with millimetre-level error under the stated noise. The Task 2 rule provides a transparent geometric baseline. The Task 3 NLP produces a bounded UR10 motion with explicit dynamics, joint limits, safety checks, and solver diagnostics. The Task 4 time-first feasible policy gives the strongest final result: 96\% full hoop-crossing success across 50 seeds at \(1.383\,\mathrm{s}\) mean successful catch time. The balanced-cost ablation shows how much accuracy one can gain by spending roughly \(0.038\,\mathrm{s}\) of extra catch time.
