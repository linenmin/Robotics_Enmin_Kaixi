\section*{Reverse-Hoops-Playing Robot Arm}

\subsection*{System Thesis and Experimental Setup}

This task admits a natural decomposition into estimation, feasibility-aware candidate selection, and constrained UR10 motion planning. The dominant design choice is the feasibility-aware selector. A better estimator alone does not solve the task, and a simple geometric catch point does not give a robot-executable catch. The experiments test this claim by holding the Kalman estimator and NLP controller fixed while changing the selection rule.

The controller plans once after candidate selection and then replays the plan open-loop. It does not run MPC inside the simulation loop. The benchmark therefore separates planner quality from execution quality: terminal TCP error measures the NLP fit to the predicted target, while radial crossing error measures the final open-loop result against the true simulated ball trajectory. The simulator includes a bounce model, but all reported catches occur before bounce. A post-bounce catch would require a switching model or an interacting multiple-model filter.

\subsection*{Trajectory Estimation under Position Noise (addresses Task 1)}

The ball state uses position and velocity,
\[
x=\begin{bmatrix}p_x&p_y&p_z&v_x&v_y&v_z\end{bmatrix}^\top .
\]
The process model integrates constant horizontal velocity and known gravity in \(z\). The measurement model observes position only. With a Gaussian prior, linear dynamics, and linear Gaussian observations, the Kalman filter gives the Bayes-optimal MMSE update and an analytic covariance. An EKF collapses to the same linear update here, and a particle filter adds sampling noise without adding modelling power.

The filter initializes from the first noisy position measurement with zero velocity and a broad velocity covariance. The robot joint state remains perfectly observable in the simulator, so the estimator only tracks the ball. Across 50 seeds at the project noise level, raw measurement RMSE was \(1.74\,\mathrm{mm}\), filtered RMSE was \(0.90\,\mathrm{mm}\), and 0.5 s future-prediction RMSE was \(3.51\,\mathrm{mm}\). The 95th percentile future-prediction RMSE was \(6.35\,\mathrm{mm}\), well below the \(30\,\mathrm{mm}\) hoop clearance.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{../outputs/task1/task1_trajectory_prediction.png}
    \caption{Kalman-filter prediction for one seed. The 50-seed validation keeps the 0.5 s prediction error below \(6.35\,\mathrm{mm}\) at the 95th percentile, far inside the \(30\,\mathrm{mm}\) clearance.}
    \label{fig:task1}
\end{figure}

A noise sweep supports the same conclusion. When measurement noise increases from 1 to 10 mm, mean 0.5 s prediction RMSE increases from \(3.67\) to \(10.34\,\mathrm{mm}\), and the maximum observed RMSE remains \(20.01\,\mathrm{mm}\). A downstream Task 4 sweep over 20 seeds keeps time-first success at 95\% for 1, 3, and 5 mm noise, and at 90\% for 10 mm. The estimator is therefore not the main bottleneck under the stated 1 mm setting.

The covariance output does not materially change selection in this benchmark. It grows monotonically over the 0.8 s prediction window, from about \(100\) to \(178\,\mathrm{mm}\) in \(\sqrt{\mathrm{tr}(P)}\), so it mostly duplicates catch-time preference rather than detecting the two failure seeds. The final selector keeps the covariance as a diagnostic for future closed-loop variants, but it does not rely on a tuned covariance threshold.

\subsection*{Geometric Candidate Selection Baselines (addresses Task 2)}

Task 2 asks for a simple interception point. The geometric baseline samples the predicted trajectory and rejects points that occur too soon, sit below \(z=0.35\,\mathrm{m}\), or lie too far from the current hoop center. It then chooses the earliest remaining point. For the validation seed, this rule selected \(t=1.36\,\mathrm{s}\) and position \([1.224,-0.119,1.074]\,\mathrm{m}\).

This rule answers where the ball could be caught in Cartesian space. It does not answer whether the UR10 can put the hoop there with the required pose and acceleration. Across 50 seeds, the simple geometric rule attempted an early catch at \(1.295\pm0.012\,\mathrm{s}\), but it produced 0 successful catches under the full NLP and hoop-crossing check.

A second baseline removes prediction completely. It uses a damped Jacobian pseudo-inverse Cartesian velocity controller,
\[
\dot q = J(q)^\top\left(J(q)J(q)^\top+\lambda^2I\right)^{-1}K(p_{\mathrm{ball,meas}}-p_{\mathrm{tcp}}),
\]
with velocity and acceleration saturation. This reactive controller also gives 0\% success. Its closest TCP-to-ball distance was \(0.176\pm0.058\,\mathrm{m}\), and even the best seed stayed \(59.4\,\mathrm{mm}\) away. Prediction is therefore necessary, and a prediction-only geometric catch point remains insufficient.

\subsection*{Constrained Trajectory Optimization for the UR10 (addresses Task 3)}

The controller solves a finite-horizon nonlinear program over joint position, velocity, and acceleration,
\[
\{q_k,\dot q_k\}_{k=0}^{N},\quad \{\ddot q_k\}_{k=0}^{N-1}.
\]
It uses the second-order discrete dynamics
\[
q_{k+1}=q_k+\dot q_k\Delta t+\tfrac{1}{2}\ddot q_k\Delta t^2,\qquad
\dot q_{k+1}=\dot q_k+\ddot q_k\Delta t .
\]
The optimization problem is
\[
\begin{aligned}
\min_{q,\dot q,\ddot q}\;&
w_p\|p_{\mathrm{tcp}}(q_N)-p^\star\|_2^2
\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} w_u\|\ddot q_k\|_2^2
\sum_{k=1}^{N} w_v\|\dot q_k\|_2^2\\
&+w_o(1-R_{zz}(q_N))^2
w_n\bigl(1-(n_{\mathrm{hoop}}(q_N)^\top\hat v_{\mathrm{ball}})^2\bigr)\\
\mathrm{s.t.}\;& q_0=q_{\mathrm{init}},\quad \dot q_0=\dot q_{\mathrm{init}},\\
& q_{\min}\le q_k\le q_{\max},\quad
-\dot q_{\max}\le \dot q_k\le \dot q_{\max},\quad
-\ddot q_{\max}\le \ddot q_k\le \ddot q_{\max},\\
& z_{\mathrm{tcp}}(q_k)\ge h_{\mathrm{table}}+m_{\mathrm{tcp}},\quad
R_{zz}(q_k)\ge0,\qquad \forall k .
\end{aligned}
\]
The validation uses \(N=28\), \(\Delta t=0.05\,\mathrm{s}\), \(w_p=1500\), \(w_u=2\times10^{-2}\), \(w_v=10^{-3}\), \(w_o=10\), and \(w_n=5\). The mesh is thin along the local \(x\)-axis, so the implementation uses local \(x\) as the hoop normal. The local \(z\)-axis defines the top-side constraint. Pinocchio supplies URDF forward kinematics. CasADi builds the symbolic NLP, and IPOPT solves it.

For the Task 2 target, the NLP reduced the hoop-target distance from \(0.731\,\mathrm{m}\) to \(0.020\,\mathrm{m}\). It solved in \(0.071\,\mathrm{s}\) and 15 IPOPT iterations on the validation run. The maximum acceleration was \(1.9996\,\mathrm{rad/s^2}\), the maximum velocity ratio was 0.640, and the minimum \(R_{zz}\) value was 0.732. The minimum TCP-table clearance was \(0.622\,\mathrm{m}\), the minimum frame-table clearance was \(0.115\,\mathrm{m}\), and the minimum self-sphere clearance was \(0.163\,\mathrm{m}\). Table clearance and hoop orientation enter the NLP as hard path constraints. Self-collision remains a post hoc sphere-based proxy.

\subsection*{Feasibility-Aware Candidate Selection (addresses Task 4)}

Task 4 asks for higher success rate and shorter interception time. The result depends on the comparison domain. The simple geometric rule attempts an earlier catch, but it has 0\% success, so its attempted time is not a successful-interception baseline. The fair time comparison uses successful feasible policies evaluated on the same candidate pool.

The selector forms a shared candidate pool from the predicted trajectory, solves the Task 3 NLP for each shortlisted candidate, and accepts only candidates that pass three checks: NLP convergence, hoop-plane crossing by the true ball trajectory, and zero safety penalty. All 396 evaluated candidate NLPs converged, but only 173 candidates passed the hoop-crossing test. This split identifies the bottleneck: the robot can solve the motion plans, but many geometrically plausible catch points do not place the hoop in a crossing-compatible pose.

{\footnotesize *Attempted catch time, not successful interception time.}

The time-first feasible policy gives the strongest answer to the Task 4 wording. It raises full-system success from 0\% to 96\% and catches \(0.052\,\mathrm{s}\) earlier than late feasible. This is about one control tick. Balanced cost keeps the same success rate and reduces mean radial error from \(12.75\) to \(9.60\,\mathrm{mm}\), but it catches \(0.040\,\mathrm{s}\) later than time-first. The covariance guard gives the same result as time-first because the propagated covariance changes mainly with horizon length, not with the lateral failure mechanism.

The open-loop model-execution gap remains small. For successful time-first catches, the NLP terminal error to the predicted target averaged \(20.39\,\mathrm{mm}\), the predicted hoop-crossing radial error averaged \(10.91\,\mathrm{mm}\), and the true-ball radial error averaged \(12.75\,\mathrm{mm}\). The mean predicted-to-true radial gap was \(4.08\,\mathrm{mm}\). This separates solver quality from execution quality without hiding the open-loop assumption.

\subsection*{Failure Analysis, Course Connection, and Reproducibility}

The two time-first failures are seeds 11 and 41. Both have high positive lateral velocity \(v_y\), close to the 95th percentile of the 50-seed distribution. Seed 41 almost succeeds, with best radial error \(33.4\,\mathrm{mm}\), while seed 11 stays far outside tolerance at \(145.2\,\mathrm{mm}\). A mitigation run with 16 candidates and a \(1.35\,\mathrm{m}\) workspace radius recovered neither seed. The remaining failures are therefore not estimator divergence or NLP non-convergence; they are lateral-trajectory geometry cases near the edge of the evaluated catch set.

    \caption{Failure mechanism for seeds 11 and 41. Both failures sit at the high-lateral-velocity edge of the seed distribution, which pushes the hoop crossing outside the \(30\,\mathrm{mm}\) tolerance.}

The method maps directly to the course material. The hard inequalities follow constraint-based robot programming from C4. The finite horizon over \(q\), \(\dot q\), and \(\ddot q\) is a direct-transcription optimal control problem from C5. The objective is a soft multi-objective formulation rather than a strict stack of tasks from C3. A hard hierarchical stack was not used because joint limits, acceleration limits, hoop orientation, and table clearance have equal physical priority once they become hard constraints. A single-step resolved acceleration controller can track a Cartesian target, but it cannot enforce the path constraints over the full prediction window. A minimum-jerk trajectory handles smoothness, but it does not handle the hoop crossing and joint-limit constraints by itself.

The experiments ran with Python 3.13.13, NumPy 2.4.3, CasADi 3.6.7, Pinocchio 3.4.0, and IPOPT through CasADi. The main benchmark uses seeds 0--49. The noise sweep uses seeds 0--49 for prediction error and seeds 0--19 for downstream success. The main reproduction commands are:
\[
\texttt{python scripts/benchmark\_high\_score\_strategies.py}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\texttt{python scripts/render\_high\_score\_figures.py}.
\]

\subsection*{Conclusion}

The final system supports the thesis. Prediction is necessary, because the reactive controller never reaches the ball within the tolerance. Prediction alone is insufficient, because the simple geometric selector also gives 0\% success. The decisive step is feasibility-aware candidate selection: it keeps the estimator and controller fixed, filters candidates through the UR10 NLP and the true hoop-crossing geometry, and raises success to 96\%. Time-first feasible gives the shortest successful catch among the evaluated policies, while balanced cost shows the price of buying a few millimetres of radial accuracy.
